    \begin{abstract}
        High-level synthesis (HLS) scheduling, binding, and resource allocation are jointly NP-hard,
        and together determine the latency--area trade-off of the generated data path. This paper
        formulates the three sub-problems as a single simulated annealing (SA) search driven by one
        scalarized latency--area cost, with the number of allocated resource instances itself treated
        as a move within the search rather than fixed in advance. A C++ simulator implementing this
        formulation -- seeded from an As-Soon-As-Possible (ASAP) schedule under a generous resource
        pool, with a shift/rebind/add-resource/remove-resource move set and a feasibility-restoring
        repair step -- is evaluated on a 515-operation arithmetic data-flow graph. Across three
        cost-weighting configurations, the ASAP-seeded starting point is consistently a local minimum
        of the cost surface, and the search wanders away from it by a similar amount during
        exploration; the weighting instead determines what the low-temperature, near-greedy phase
        finds once it arrives -- negligible further gain when latency dominates the cost, versus a
        sharp late collapse to a much lower cost when area dominates. This shows that the
        $\alpha/\beta$ weighting reshapes not just the final answer, but the shape of the search
        itself.
    \end{abstract}

        high-level synthesis, simulated annealing, scheduling, resource allocation, resource binding,

    \section{Introduction}
    \label{sec:introduction}

    High-level synthesis (HLS) compiles a behavioral description into a register-transfer-level
    data path by solving three interdependent sub-problems: \emph{scheduling} assigns each
    operation of a data-flow graph (DFG) to a control step, \emph{binding} maps each scheduled
    operation to a concrete functional-unit instance, and \emph{allocation} decides how many
    instances of each resource type the data path actually contains. All three choices interact
    through the same resource-usage pattern over time, and jointly they are NP-hard, since the
    problem subsumes resource-constrained scheduling. Classical HLS tools sidestep this
    complexity with fast constructive heuristics -- As-Soon-As-Possible (ASAP), As-Late-As-Possible
    (ALAP), and List Scheduling -- that build a single schedule greedily, cycle by cycle. These
    methods are optimal only under restrictive assumptions (e.g.\ unlimited resources, or a fixed
    priority order that happens to match the true critical path) and, being purely constructive,
    cannot revise an earlier decision once made; they therefore get trapped in local optima of the
    latency--area cost surface.

    Simulated annealing (SA) is a natural fit for escaping this trap: unlike greedy list
    scheduling, it accepts \emph{worsening} moves with a temperature-controlled probability, which
    lets the search leave local optima that a monotonically improving heuristic cannot escape, while
    still converging to a stable solution as the temperature is lowered. SA-based data-path synthesis
    also has direct precedent in VLSI CAD, where it was used to jointly allocate registers,
    functional units, and interconnect \cite{Devadas1989}. This places SA in contrast to exact
    formulations such as Integer Linear Programming (ILP), which can certify optimality but whose
    solve time grows too quickly with problem size to remain practical on realistic DFGs.

    This paper makes the following contributions:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item a joint scheduling + binding + allocation SA formulation driven by a single weighted
        cost function (Section~\ref{sec:math-basics});
        \item a concrete SA move set and repair mechanism that keeps every candidate schedule
        feasible after each move (Section~\ref{sec:algorithm});
        \item a worst-case complexity analysis of the scheduler's per-move cost and total
        search cost across all runs (Section~\ref{sec:overhead});
        \item an empirical study of how the $\alpha$/$\beta$ latency--area weighting reshapes the
        search landscape around the ASAP-seeded starting point, on a realistic DFG benchmark
        (Section~\ref{sec:simulation}).
    \end{itemize}

    The remainder of the paper first reviews related scheduling and allocation approaches
    (Section~\ref{sec:related-work}), then formalizes the problem
    (Section~\ref{sec:problem-statement}), before presenting the SA formulation, algorithm,
    complexity analysis, and experiments in turn.

    \section{Related Work}
    \label{sec:related-work}

    Classical HLS tools schedule with fast constructive heuristics such as ASAP, ALAP, and List
    Scheduling, or with the more refined Force-Directed Scheduling, which balances concurrency across
    control steps to reduce resource cost under a fixed latency \cite{DeMicheli1994,PaulinKnight1989};
    exact formulations based on integer linear programming can certify optimal schedules but scale
    poorly with problem size \cite{HwangLeeHsu1991}. Simulated annealing has previously been used to
    jointly allocate registers, functional units, and interconnect in data-path synthesis
    \cite{Devadas1989}, and other metaheuristics such as genetic algorithms have likewise been applied
    to combined HLS scheduling and allocation \cite{Heijligers1995}. SA is not specific to HLS: the
    same accept-worsening-moves-under-a-cooling-schedule mechanism underlies metaheuristic solvers for
    other combinatorial optimization problems, such as vehicle routing \cite{Blocho2020}, which
    suggests the search dynamics studied here are a property of SA as an optimization method rather
    than an artifact particular to data-path synthesis. This paper follows the HLS-specific SA-based
    line of work, with one difference: the number of allocated resource instances is itself a move
    within the same search, rather than being fixed in advance or optimized in a separate pass.

    \section{Problem Statement}
    \label{sec:problem-statement}

    \textbf{Input.} The input is a data-flow graph $G = (V, E)$, where each vertex $v \in V$ is a
    typed operation (e.g.\ \texttt{LOAD}, \texttt{STORE}, \texttt{ADD}, \texttt{SUB}, \texttt{MUL},
    \texttt{DIV}), and each edge $(u, v) \in E$ denotes a data dependency requiring $u$ to complete
    before $v$ begins. A resource library specifies, for each resource type, an area cost, a fixed
    latency in clock cycles, and an available instance count.

    \textbf{Decision variables.} A candidate solution assigns to every operation $v \in V$ a start
    cycle $c(v) \in \mathbb{N}$ and a resource-instance binding $r(v)$, identifying which physical
    instance of $v$'s resource type executes it. A solution is \emph{feasible} iff it satisfies:
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item \textbf{Precedence:} for every edge $(u, v) \in E$,
        $c(v) \geq c(u) + \mathrm{latency}(u)$;
        \item \textbf{Resource exclusivity:} for any two operations $v_1 \neq v_2$ with
        $r(v_1) = r(v_2)$, their execution intervals
        $[c(v), c(v) + \mathrm{latency}(v))$ do not overlap.
    \end{enumerate}

    \textbf{Objective.} Informally, the goal is to find a feasible $(c, r)$, together with an
    instance count per resource type, that minimizes a weighted combination of total schedule
    latency and total allocated area; the precise weighted cost function is defined in
    Section~\ref{sec:math-basics}.

    \textbf{Complexity.} The joint problem is NP-hard, since fixing the resource counts and binding
    already reduces it to resource-constrained scheduling, itself a well-known NP-hard problem. Here
    allocation is not fixed but part of the search, so the combined search space -- cycle
    assignment $\times$ binding $\times$ resource count -- is strictly larger still. Even for DFGs of
    modest size, the number of feasible schedules grows far too quickly for exhaustive or exact
    search to remain practical, which motivates the simulated-annealing-based approach developed in
    Sections~\ref{sec:math-basics} and~\ref{sec:algorithm}.

    \section{Mathematical Foundations}
    \label{sec:math-basics}

    \subsection{Cost Function}
    \label{sec:cost-function}

    Every candidate solution $(c, r, k)$ from Section~\ref{sec:problem-statement} is scored by a
    single scalar cost,
    \begin{equation}
        \text{Cost}(c, r, k) = \alpha \cdot L(c) + \beta \cdot A(k),
        \label{eq:cost}
    \end{equation}
    combining two terms. The latency term $L(c) = \max_{v \in V} \big(c(v) + \mathrm{latency}(v)\big)$
    is the schedule length in clock cycles, i.e.\ the completion time of the last operation. The area
    term $A(k) = \sum_{t} k_t \cdot \mathrm{area}(t)$ is the total hardware cost of the allocated
    resource pool, summing the unit area of each resource type $t$ over its allocated instance count
    $k_t$; note that area depends only on how many instances are allocated, not on how many operations
    are bound to them. The weights $\alpha, \beta \geq 0$ let a designer trade latency against area:
    Equation~\eqref{eq:cost} is a linear scalarization of the underlying two-objective
    (latency, area) problem into the single objective that the search of Section~\ref{sec:algorithm}
    minimizes, and different choices of $\alpha/\beta$ correspond to different preferences on that
    trade-off.

    \subsection{Simulated Annealing Formalism}
    \label{sec:sa-formalism}

    Simulated annealing explores the space of feasible solutions by repeatedly proposing a
    neighboring solution and deciding whether to move there. Let $\text{Cost}_{\text{cur}}$ be the
    cost of the current solution and $\text{Cost}_{\text{next}}$ the cost of a proposed neighbor, and
    define $\Delta = \text{Cost}_{\text{next}} - \text{Cost}_{\text{cur}}$. The neighbor is accepted
    with the Metropolis probability
    \begin{equation}
        P(\Delta, T) =
        \begin{cases}
            1, & \Delta \leq 0, \\
            e^{-\Delta / T}, & \Delta > 0,
        \end{cases}
        \label{eq:metropolis}
    \end{equation}
    where $T$ is the current \emph{temperature}: improving moves are always accepted, while worsening
    moves are accepted with a probability that shrinks as $\Delta$ grows or $T$ falls. Temperature is
    lowered geometrically,
    \begin{equation}
        T_{k+1} = \gamma \cdot T_k, \qquad 0 < \gamma < 1,
        \label{eq:cooling}
    \end{equation}
    starting from an initial temperature $T_0$ and stopping once $T$ drops below a minimum threshold
    $T_{\min}$. At high temperature, Equation~\eqref{eq:metropolis} accepts almost any move, so the
    search explores broadly and can escape local optima of the cost surface; as $T \to T_{\min}$, only
    improving or mildly worsening moves survive, so the search behaves increasingly like a greedy
    local search and settles into a minimum. A slow enough (e.g.\ logarithmic) cooling schedule is
    known to converge to a global optimum in the limit, but at a runtime cost that grows accordingly;
    the finite, geometric schedule of Equation~\eqref{eq:cooling} trades that asymptotic guarantee for
    a bounded number of iterations, which we return to in Section~\ref{sec:overhead}.

    \section{Simulated Annealing Scheduling Algorithm}
    \label{sec:algorithm}

    \subsection{State Representation and Initialization}
    \label{sec:sa-state}

    A candidate solution is represented directly as the triple $(c, r, k)$ from
    Section~\ref{sec:problem-statement}: a start cycle $c(v)$ for every operation, a resource-instance
    binding $r(v)$, and a resource pool $k = (k_t)_t$ giving the number of allocated instances per
    resource type. The search is initialized with an ASAP schedule computed under the \emph{full}
    resource pool declared in the configuration -- every available instance of every type, not just
    the minimum needed. Each operation is placed at the earliest cycle its predecessors allow and
    greedily bound to a free instance, with the repair mechanism of Section~\ref{sec:sa-moves}
    resolving any remaining conflicts. This initial solution is feasible by construction and, because
    the pool is generous, typically already close to latency-optimal; it is far from cost-optimal in
    another sense, however, since every declared instance counts toward its area whether the schedule
    actually uses it or not. Section~\ref{sec:sim-process} returns to this starting point in detail, as
    it turns out to matter for how the search behaves.

    \subsection{Neighbor Generation and Repair}
    \label{sec:sa-moves}

    At each step, a neighboring solution is produced by applying one randomly chosen move to the
    current solution:
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item \textbf{Move forward}: shift a randomly chosen operation one cycle later.
        \item \textbf{Move backward}: shift a randomly chosen operation one cycle earlier (clamped at
        cycle $0$).
        \item \textbf{Rebind}: reassign a randomly chosen operation to a different, compatible
        instance of its resource type.
        \item \textbf{Add resource}: increase $k_t$ by one for a chosen resource type $t$.
        \item \textbf{Remove resource}: decrease $k_t$ by one for a chosen resource type $t$ (never
        below one), automatically rebinding any operations that were using the removed instance.
    \end{enumerate}
    A move can, on its own, break one of the two feasibility conditions of
    Section~\ref{sec:problem-statement} -- for example moving an operation earlier than one of its
    predecessors, or binding two operations to the same instance at overlapping cycles. Every move is
    therefore followed by a \emph{repair} step: \emph{dependency repair} recursively pushes an
    operation's successors forward whenever precedence is violated, and \emph{resource-overlap repair}
    shifts operations forward whenever two operations sharing an instance now overlap in time. Because
    repair always restores a feasible solution, the Metropolis test of
    Equation~\eqref{eq:metropolis} can be applied directly to the resulting cost, without any
    additional penalty term for infeasibility.

    \subsection{Acceptance, Cooling, and Multi-Run Strategy}
    \label{sec:sa-accept}

    Algorithm~\ref{alg:sas} summarizes the full outer loop. At each temperature level, a fixed number
    of neighbors are proposed, repaired, and accepted or rejected via Equation~\eqref{eq:metropolis};
    the best solution found so far is tracked separately from the current solution, since a worsening
    move may still be accepted along the way. Once the proposals at a given temperature are exhausted,
    the temperature is cooled with Equation~\eqref{eq:cooling} and the process repeats until $T$ falls
    below $T_{\min}$. Because the outcome of a single run depends on its random sequence of proposed
    moves, the scheduler performs several independent runs from different random seeds and reports the
    best solution found across all of them.

    \begin{algorithm}[t]
        \caption{Simulated Annealing Scheduling (SAS)}
        \label{alg:sas}
        \begin{algorithmic}[1]
            \REQUIRE DFG $G = (V, E)$, resource library, $T_0$, $T_{\min}$, $\gamma$, iterations per
            temperature level $I$
            \ENSURE best solution found $S^\ast$
            \STATE $S \leftarrow \text{ASAP}(G)$ with full resource pool
            \STATE $S^\ast \leftarrow S$
            \STATE $T \leftarrow T_0$
            \WHILE{$T > T_{\min}$}
                \FOR{$i = 1$ to $I$}
                    \STATE $S' \leftarrow \text{Repair}(\text{Move}(S))$
                    \STATE $\Delta \leftarrow \text{Cost}(S') - \text{Cost}(S)$
                    \IF{$\Delta \leq 0$ \OR $\text{rand}(0,1) < e^{-\Delta/T}$}
                        \STATE $S \leftarrow S'$
                        \IF{$\text{Cost}(S) < \text{Cost}(S^\ast)$}
                            \STATE $S^\ast \leftarrow S$
                        \ENDIF
                    \ENDIF
                \ENDFOR
                \STATE $T \leftarrow \gamma \cdot T$
            \ENDWHILE
            \RETURN $S^\ast$
        \end{algorithmic}
    \end{algorithm}

    \section{Runtime and Overhead Analysis}
    \label{sec:overhead}

    \subsection{Theoretical Complexity}
    \label{sec:overhead-theory}

    Generating a candidate neighbor is cheap: four of the five move types touch a single operation
    and run in $O(1)$, while rebind is $O(m_t)$ in the number of available instances $m_t$ of the
    relevant resource type, which is normally small. The repair step dominates instead: dependency
    repair can, in the worst case, propagate through every remaining successor of the moved operation,
    and resource-overlap repair can likewise touch every operation sharing that instance, so a single
    move-and-repair step costs $O(|V| + |E|)$ in the worst case. At a given temperature, $I$ such steps
    are performed, and the number of temperature levels visited before $T$ drops below $T_{\min}$ is
    $\lceil \log_\gamma (T_{\min} / T_0) \rceil$. Repeating this for a number of independent runs
    $R$, the total worst-case work is
    \begin{equation}
        O\Big(R \cdot \big\lceil \log_\gamma (T_{\min}/T_0) \big\rceil \cdot I \cdot (|V| + |E|)\Big).
        \label{eq:complexity}
    \end{equation}
    Every factor in Equation~\eqref{eq:complexity} except the graph size $|V| + |E|$ is a
    user-controlled parameter of the search, which makes the runtime-quality trade-off of
    Section~\ref{sec:sa-formalism} explicit and tunable, at the cost of no longer offering an
    asymptotic optimality guarantee.

    \subsection{Overhead in Practice}
    \label{sec:overhead-empirical}

    The bound in Equation~\eqref{eq:complexity} is a worst case dominated by graph size and the
    number of accepted iterations; it is not a tight estimate of actual runtime, since a repair step
    rarely needs to touch the whole graph in practice -- a moved operation's precedence and
    resource conflicts are typically local to a small neighborhood of the DFG, not the whole
    graph, so the per-move cost seen in practice is usually far below the $O(|V|+|E|)$ bound it is
    measured against.

    \section{Simulation}
    \label{sec:simulation}

    The concepts of Sections~\ref{sec:math-basics} and~\ref{sec:algorithm} are implemented in a C++ simulator built around the model of
    Section~\ref{sec:problem-statement}. This section walks through what the simulator consumes and
    produces, how its starting point is obtained, and then makes one observation about how the search
    behaves as the cost function's weights change. Development of the C++ simulator was assisted by AI tools
    \cite{Anthropic_Claude_2026, OpenAI_ChatGPT_2026}.

    \subsection{Input: Data-Flow Graph and Configuration}
    \label{sec:sim-input}

    The simulator takes two inputs. The DFG is a flat text file, one operation per line, giving an
    operation's type and its operand identifiers; dependencies are inferred directly from those
    operand references. The benchmark DFG used throughout this section follows this pattern:
    \begin{verbatim}
t1   = LOAD(D1A1)
t9   = LOAD(D1B1)
t17  = MUL(t1, t9)
t18  = MUL(t2, t10)
t25  = ADD(t17, t18)
...
t511 = ADD(t510, t509)
t515 = DIV(t511, t514)
STORE(t515)
    \end{verbatim}
    The full graph repeats this load--multiply--reduce pattern across several input vectors, combines
    the resulting partial sums through further addition and subtraction stages, and finishes with a
    division and a store, for 515 operations over the six operation types \texttt{LOAD}, \texttt{STORE},
    \texttt{ADD}, \texttt{SUB}, \texttt{MUL}, and \texttt{DIV}.

    Alongside the DFG, a JSON configuration fixes the SA parameters and the resource library:
    \begingroup\fontsize{7}{9}\selectfont
    \begin{verbatim}
{
  "sa": {
    "initial_temperature": 1,
    "cooling_rate": 0.95,
    "min_temperature": 0.005,
    "iterations_per_temp": 50,
    "runs": 10,
    "alpha": 0.75,
    "beta": 0.25,
    "seed": 6324798
  },
  "resources": {
    "LOAD":  {"count": 25, "area": 5,  "latency": 1},
    "STORE": {"count": 25, "area": 5,  "latency": 1},
    "ADD":   {"count": 25, "area": 5,  "latency": 1},
    "SUB":   {"count": 25, "area": 5,  "latency": 1},
    "MUL":   {"count": 25, "area": 15, "latency": 3},
    "DIV":   {"count": 25, "area": 40, "latency": 4}
  }
}
    \end{verbatim}
    \endgroup
    \texttt{initial\_temperature}, \texttt{min\_temperature}, and \texttt{cooling\_rate} are $T_0$,
    $T_{\min}$, and $\gamma$ from Equation~\eqref{eq:cooling}; \texttt{iterations\_per\_temp} is $I$
    and \texttt{runs} is the number of independent restarts $R$, both from
    Algorithm~\ref{alg:sas}; \texttt{alpha} and \texttt{beta} are the cost-function weights of
    Equation~\eqref{eq:cost} -- the two values varied in Section~\ref{sec:sim-tradeoff}; and
    \texttt{seed} is a base value from which each of the $R$ runs derives its own, distinct seed (an
    offset of the run index), so restarts are independent random streams while the whole experiment
    stays reproducible. Under \texttt{resources},
    each entry instantiates one resource type from Section~\ref{sec:problem-statement}: \texttt{count}
    is the maximum number of instances that may ever be allocated, \texttt{area} their unit area cost,
    and \texttt{latency} their fixed execution latency in cycles.

    \subsection{Output: The Resulting Schedule}
    \label{sec:sim-output}

    The simulator's output is a concrete assignment of every operation to a resource instance and a
    cycle interval -- the $(c, r, k)$ triple of Section~\ref{sec:sa-state}, made explicit. An excerpt
    of the best schedule found for the benchmark DFG:
    \begin{table}[h]
        \centering
        \caption{Excerpt of the resulting schedule (best solution found).}
        \label{tab:gantt}
        \begin{tabular}{lccc}
            \toprule
            Resource & Start & End & Operation \\
            \midrule
            \texttt{Res\_7\_LOAD} & 4 & 5 & \texttt{t1} \\
            \texttt{Res\_7\_LOAD} & 0 & 1 & \texttt{t2} \\
            \texttt{Res\_3\_MUL}  & 6 & 9 & \texttt{t17} \\
            \texttt{Res\_1\_ADD}  & 9 & 10 & \texttt{t25} \\
            \bottomrule
        \end{tabular}
    \end{table}
    Each row fixes the start cycle $c(v)$, the binding $r(v)$ -- a named instance, not just a
    resource type -- and its cycle interval $[c(v), c(v) + \mathrm{latency}(v))$. The set of distinct
    instance names appearing in the table, grouped by type, is the allocated pool $k$. Read cycle by
    cycle, this table is exactly the control-step schedule a downstream HLS back end would use to
    drive the datapath's control logic.

    \subsection{From ASAP Baseline to Optimized Schedule}
    \label{sec:sim-process}

    Simulated annealing does not start from an arbitrary or random solution: the initial state
    $S_0$ handed to Algorithm~\ref{alg:sas} is the ASAP-under-full-pool schedule described in
    Section~\ref{sec:sa-state}, instantiated here on the benchmark DFG. Because the declared pool
    is generous relative to the parallelism actually present in this DFG, $S_0$ is already close to
    latency-optimal -- there is little room left to shorten the schedule further -- while its area
    is essentially the configuration's maximum, since every declared instance is allocated whether
    or not the schedule uses it, leaving a large part of the initial resource pool idle.

    This asymmetry -- an already-good latency paired with a deliberately wasteful area -- is not an
    incidental implementation detail. As Section~\ref{sec:sim-tradeoff} shows, it directly shapes how
    the search behaves once $\alpha$ and $\beta$ change how costly that idle area is judged to be.

    \subsection{Cost-versus-Temperature Landscape}
    \label{sec:sim-tradeoff}

    At each temperature level $T$, a run's $I$ proposals at that level end with the search in some
    particular current solution -- exactly the solution that is carried forward, unchanged, into the
    next, cooler level. Rather than summarizing everything that happened during those $I$ proposals,
    each run records only this single \emph{resulting cost}: one number per run, per temperature
    level. Aggregating this resulting cost across all $R$ independent runs at a given temperature then
    yields three quantities: the \emph{best} (the minimum resulting cost across runs), the
    \emph{worst} (the maximum resulting cost across runs), and the \emph{mean} (the average resulting
    cost across runs). Every run also uses its own random seed (Section~\ref{sec:sim-input}), so the
    spread between best and worst reflects genuine run-to-run variation and not a shared random
    sequence. Plotting these three quantities against temperature -- with temperature decreasing along
    the axis, so the plot reads left to right from $T_0$ down to $T_{\min}$, start to end of the
    run -- is the tool used below to visualize how the search behaves.

    Figures~\ref{fig:tradeoff-075}--\ref{fig:tradeoff-025} show this plot for three configurations
    that differ only in $\alpha$ and $\beta$: latency-optimizing ($\alpha=0.75,\beta=0.25$), balanced
    ($\alpha=0.5,\beta=0.5$), and area-optimizing ($\alpha=0.25,\beta=0.75$), all else held fixed.

    The one point these three plots are meant to make is not about the mid-temperature exploration
    phase, which looks similar across all three weightings: in every configuration, $S_0$ is a local
    minimum of the cost surface, and the resulting cost climbs by a comparable amount as the search
    first moves away from it, since exploration at high temperature accepts worsening moves more or
    less irrespective of which term of Equation~\eqref{eq:cost} is doing the charging. The difference
    shows up only in the low-temperature endgame. As $T$ approaches $T_{\min}$, Equation~\eqref{eq:metropolis}
    admits essentially only moves with $\Delta \leq 0$, so the search enters a near-greedy phase in
    which the only moves that survive are ones that lower cost -- chiefly, shedding one of $S_0$'s many
    idle resource instances. Under the latency-optimizing weighting, that near-greedy phase has little
    to gain: removing an idle instance barely moves a cost dominated by the large $\alpha$ term, so the
    run ends close to, or even above, where it started -- $S_0$ is, for practical purposes, the deepest
    point the search reaches (Fig.~\ref{fig:tradeoff-075}). As $\beta$ grows, the same shedding moves
    are worth far more, and the near-greedy phase has correspondingly more to gain: the final stretch
    of cooling turns from a negligible correction into a sharp collapse that ends well below the
    starting cost (Fig.~\ref{fig:tradeoff-025}), with the balanced weighting falling in between
    (Fig.~\ref{fig:tradeoff-050}).

    Two caveats apply when reading these three plots side by side. First, the cost function itself
    changes between them, so the absolute cost values on the $y$-axis are not directly comparable
    across figures -- only the \emph{shape} of each curve, relative to its own starting cost, carries
    meaning. Second, that shape is not merely descriptive: it feeds back into the search through the
    Metropolis criterion of Equation~\eqref{eq:metropolis}, since the acceptance probability depends
    on $\Delta$ in the very units these plots show. Read this way, the three configurations do not
    differ in how far the search wanders during exploration, but in what the near-greedy endgame finds
    once it arrives: a latency-optimizing weighting leaves $S_0$ as effectively the deepest point
    reachable, while an area-optimizing weighting reveals a much deeper basin that the search only
    falls into in the last few temperature steps before $T_{\min}$.

    \section{Conclusion}
    \label{sec:conclusion}

    Across all three $\alpha/\beta$ configurations, the ASAP-seeded starting point $S_0$ is a local
    minimum of the cost surface: during the mid-temperature exploration phase, the resulting cost
    per run rises away from $S_0$ by a comparable amount regardless of weighting. What differs is
    the low-temperature endgame, once the Metropolis criterion effectively admits only strictly
    improving moves. Under the latency-optimizing weighting, that near-greedy phase finds little to
    gain -- shedding idle resource instances barely moves a cost dominated by the large $\alpha$
    term -- so the run ends at or above where it started, with $S_0$ effectively the best point the
    search reaches. As $\beta$ grows, the same shedding moves pay off far more, and the final
    descent turns from negligible to dramatic: under the area-optimizing weighting, the last stretch
    of cooling collapses the cost well below its starting value. The $\alpha/\beta$ weighting
    therefore does not change how far the search wanders during exploration -- it changes whether a
    much deeper basin exists to fall into once the search turns greedy.

    These results come with several limitations. The evaluation uses a single benchmark DFG, so
    generalization of this landscape shape to graphs with substantially different topology,
    resource mixes, or scale (e.g.\ CHStone or MachSuite kernels) remains untested. The
    comparison is internal to the SA search itself: no quantitative baseline against ASAP, ALAP,
    list scheduling, or force-directed scheduling is reported, so the absolute quality of the
    schedules found -- not just their behavior under different weightings -- is not yet
    established. The cooling schedule is a fixed geometric decay used for all three
    configurations; an adaptive or reheating schedule might behave differently on the very
    landscapes this paper characterizes. Finally, the SA formulation is compared against neither
    other metaheuristics (genetic algorithms, tabu search, particle swarm optimization) nor an
    exact ILP solution on a small-scale instance that could bound the optimality gap.
